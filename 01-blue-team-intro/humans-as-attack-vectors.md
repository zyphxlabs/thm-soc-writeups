# Humans as Attack Vectors — Writeup

## What This Room Was About
This room was about why humans are considered the weakest link
in cybersecurity and how attackers take advantage of that.
Instead of breaking through firewalls, attackers just trick people.

## What I Learned

### Why Humans Are Targeted
Humans have access — to systems, emails, databases.
Attackers go after humans because it is faster and easier
than spending days trying to exploit technical vulnerabilities.

### Social Engineering
This is the technique of manipulating people instead of hacking systems.
For it to work it has to look trustworthy and trigger an emotion
like urgency or fear that makes the victim act without thinking.

### Attack Types I Learned About
Phishing — fake emails with malicious links. 3.4 billion sent every day.
Malware Downloads — fake apps disguised as real ones.
Deepfakes — AI generated video calls impersonating real people.
A finance worker was tricked into sending 25 million dollars this way.
Impersonation — attacker calls pretending to be IT support.
Others — USB drops, insider threats, fake job offers.

### Mitigation vs Detection
Mitigation stops attacks before they happen.
Detection catches what gets through.
You always need both because no mitigation is perfect.

Ways to mitigate:
- Anti phishing solution to block malicious emails automatically
- Antivirus and EDR on every device
- Train employees to verify suspicious requests
- Run phishing simulations to test awareness

## What I Did in the Lab
Opened the SOC dashboard and worked through two tabs.

Employees at Risk:
Reviewed flagged employees and took steps to protect each one.

Security Policy:
Implemented policies to block phishing emails company wide.

## My Key Takeaway
SOC is not just about watching alerts on a screen.
A good SOC analyst understands human behavior too.
Most attacks start with a human mistake not a technical flaw.
Protecting the people is just as important as protecting the systems.
