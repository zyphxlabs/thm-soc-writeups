## Humans Are the Easiest Target

So instead of spending days trying to break through firewalls or finding some zero day vulnerability, an attacker just... tricks a person. because that person already has access to everything — emails, internal systems, databases, all of it. why do hard technical stuff when you can just manipulate the one who already has the keys.

## Social Engineering

this is basically hacking a human instead of a machine. for it to work two things have to be there — it has to look real enough that the person trusts it, and it has to make them feel something, usually fear or urgency, so they just act without thinking. that mix of trust and panic is what makes it dangerous.

## How These Attacks Actually Look

Phishing is fake emails with bad links inside them, and 3.4 billion of these go out every single day. that number alone should tell you how common this is. then there are malware downloads which are fake apps that look completely normal, you download what you think is some utility or game and something harmful is running in the background without you knowing.

deepfakes are the scary new one — attacker uses AI to make a fake video call while pretending to be someone real. there was an actual case where a finance worker got on a video call, thought he was talking to his colleagues, and ended up sending 25 million dollars to an attacker. just like that.

other ways this happens:

- impersonation — acting like someone else, copying their identity so people trust you
- USB drops — infected drive left somewhere on purpose, someone picks it up, plugs it in, done
- insider threats — someone already inside the company with bad intentions, already has access so much harder to catch
- fake job offers — pretend to recruit someone to get their info or make them click something

## Mitigation vs Detection

These two are different things and both matter. mitigation is stopping the attack before it even reaches you. detection is catching what already got through. you need both because no system is perfect, there is always going to be some gap somewhere.

- anti phishing tools that block bad emails before they even land in inbox
- antivirus and EDR running on every device
- train employees so they actually know what suspicious looks like
- run fake phishing tests to check if that training is doing anything

## As an L1 Analyst

Most attacks don't start with some crazy technical exploit, they start with a normal person making a normal mistake. so sitting there just staring at alerts all day isn't enough, you have to understand people too. keeping people aware and safe is just as much part of the job as keeping the systems locked down.
